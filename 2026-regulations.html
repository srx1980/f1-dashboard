2026 F1 Regulations Explained

The 2026 Formula 1 season introduces the biggest rules overhaul in a generation, combining an all-new power unit, a switch to fully sustainable fuel, active aerodynamics, and smaller, lighter cars. This guide breaks down what actually changes, and why it matters for the racing.

New power units: a 50/50 hybrid

The 2026 power unit keeps the 1.6-litre V6 turbo-hybrid architecture but rebalances it to roughly a 50% internal combustion / 50% electric split. Electrical output rises almost threefold to around 350 kW (about 470 hp), delivered through the MGU-K.

  • MGU-H removed: the complex heat-recovery unit is dropped to cut cost and simplify the engines, helping attract new manufacturers.
  • More electric deployment: significantly greater energy recovery and deployment than the outgoing rules.
  • Combined output stays in the region of 1,000 hp.

100% sustainable fuel

From 2026, F1 runs on 100% sustainable fuel made from non-food sources, e-fuels, or municipal waste. It is a cornerstone of the sport's aim to be Net Zero Carbon, and a technology F1 wants to see transfer to road cars.

Active aerodynamics and overtaking

Cars gain active aerodynamics with movable front and rear wings. Drivers switch between a high-downforce Z-mode for corners and a low-drag X-mode for straights, improving efficiency to suit the new power unit.

  • Less downforce and drag: roughly 30% less downforce and around 55% less drag than the previous generation of cars.
  • Manual Override Mode: replaces DRS. A chasing driver gets an extra burst of electrical energy to help complete an overtake, rather than relying on a rear-wing slot alone.

Smaller, lighter, more nimble cars

The 2026 cars are deliberately more compact to improve agility and reduce the "oversized car" trend of recent years.

  • Narrower: maximum width cut by 100 mm (from 2000 mm to 1900 mm).
  • Shorter: maximum wheelbase reduced to 3400 mm.
  • Lighter: minimum weight cut by around 30 kg to about 768 kg.
  • Slimmer tyres: narrower front and rear tyres, keeping the 18-inch wheels.

New teams and manufacturers

The grid grows and the engine line-up changes. Audi enters as a works manufacturer (taking over the Sauber operation), while Cadillac joins as an 11th team. Combined with the new power-unit rules, 2026 attracts one of the most diverse manufacturer fields in modern F1 history.

Sprint weekends

The Sprint format continues broadly as before, with a small number of race weekends featuring a shorter Saturday race that sets its own grid and awards points, separate from the main Grand Prix.
