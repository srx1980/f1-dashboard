2026 F1 Regulations Explained

The 2026 Formula 1 season introduces the biggest rules overhaul in a generation. An all-new power unit, a switch to fully sustainable fuel, active aerodynamics, and smaller, lighter cars reshape every single aspect of the car. This guide breaks down what actually changes, and why it matters for the racing.

New power units: a 50/50 hybrid

The 2026 power unit keeps the 1.6-litre V6 turbo-hybrid architecture but rebalances it to roughly a 50% internal combustion / 50% electric split. Electrical output rises almost threefold to around 350 kW (470 hp), delivered through the MGU-K, while the combustion engine's contribution drops to about 400 kW (540 hp) — the combined output stays in the region of 1,000 hp.

  • MGU-H removed: the complex heat-recovery unit is dropped to cut cost and simplify the engines, helping attract new manufacturers.
  • More electric deployment: the MGU-K output jumps from 160 hp to 470 hp, and the power units are expected to recover twice as much electrical energy as before.
  • Fuel flow by energy: fuel flow is now measured and limited by energy content, not by mass — a change that aligns the rules with the new sustainable fuel.

100% sustainable fuel

From 2026, F1 runs on 100% sustainable fuel made from non-food sources, e-fuels, or municipal waste. It is a cornerstone of the sport's aim to be Net Zero Carbon by 2030, and a technology F1 wants to see transfer to road cars.

Active aerodynamics: X-mode and Z-mode

Cars gain active aerodynamics with movable front and rear wings — a first for Formula 1. Drivers switch between two configurations:

  • Z-mode (corner mode): wings in their upright, closed position, generating maximum downforce for cornering grip.
  • X-mode (straight mode): wings rotate to a flat, open position, trading downforce for a big reduction in drag and higher top speed.

The FIA confirmed a downforce reduction of around 15% compared to the 2022–2025 generation of cars — significantly less than the 40%+ originally proposed, revised for performance and safety reasons. The new cars are expected to be roughly two seconds per lap slower than their predecessors.

  • Reduced ground-effect floor: the floor is revised to ease the porpoising issues that affected the 2022–2025 cars.
  • Manual Override Mode: replaces DRS. A chasing driver gets an extra burst of electrical energy to help complete an overtake, rather than relying on a rear-wing slot alone. Deployment is allowed when within one second of the car ahead, similar to the old DRS rules.

Smaller, lighter, more nimble cars

The 2026 cars are deliberately more compact to improve agility and reverse the "oversized car" trend of recent years.

  • Narrower: maximum width cut by 100 mm (from 2000 mm to 1900 mm).
  • Shorter: maximum wheelbase reduced from 3600 mm to 3400 mm.
  • Lighter: minimum weight cut by around 30 kg.
  • Slimmer tyres: narrower front (−25 mm) and rear (−30 mm) tyres, keeping the 18-inch wheels.

New teams and manufacturer line-up

The grid grows to 11 teams and the engine landscape shifts significantly:

  • Audi enters as a full works manufacturer, having acquired the Sauber operation in 2024. They bring their own power unit.
  • Cadillac joins as the 11th team — the first new entrant since 2016 — powered by Ferrari engines.
  • Honda → Aston Martin: Honda, now through its Honda Racing Corporation subsidiary, ends its Red Bull partnership and becomes the exclusive works supplier for Aston Martin.
  • Ford → Red Bull: Ford returns to Formula 1 for the first time since 2004, partnering with Red Bull Racing on their power unit programme.

Sprint weekends

The Sprint format continues broadly as before, with a small number of race weekends featuring a shorter Saturday race that sets its own grid and awards points, separate from the main Grand Prix.
